Pedagogical Approaches in Computer Science Education

Self Directed Learning

Self-Directed learning refers to a learning process whereby students formulate and construct knowledge on their own, without the assistance of an educator. It has been widely used in many aspects in education, more notably during Junior Cycle Classroom Based Assessments (CBA’s) and Leaving Certificate individual projects. Self directed learning in itself can give rise to many new learning opportunities within Computer Science education which may not exist in traditional subjects. For example, presenting a problem to students and asking them to find the problem and create the solution. Many students will travel along different learning pathways to lead to the same learning outcome.

In addition, self directed learning can be enhanced through the use of self assessment strategies and the incorporation of success criteria for each student. Allowing students to self-assess (with guidance and training from the educator) can enrich the learning experience and help provide more clarity regarding gaps of knowledge.

Opportunities/Benefits

  • Allows the self development of skills
  • Can increase student grit and determination levels
  • Students take ownership of their work and set clear learning goals
  • Encourages students to follow their own learning journey
  • Relates to students individual interests


Potential Challenges

  • Teacher knowledge and understanding of self directed learning
  • Student reliance on educator (depending on teacher input)
  • Possible lack of direction for weaker students
  • Collating the learning journey for each student may prove challenging
  • Student motivation and/or perseverance may fluctuate depending on ability levels

Social Learning Theory

Vygotsky’s social learning theory (1978) encompasses learning as a predominantly social process. His theory is based around social interactions, the more knowledgeable other (MKO) and the zone of proximal development (ZPD). Vygotsky believed that social interactions can aid with the process of cognitive development. Language and communication can play a vital role here too, not only within an educational setting, but at home with family and friends. The concept of social learning can expand to many ability levels. The More Knowledgeable Other (MKO) - A student's learning may be limited when working individually but the cognitive development can be raised to a significantly higher level with the aid of another individual. This untapping of potential can be seen in many activities whereby students begin assignments individually but assess one another as the progress throughout their learning. This provides a platform of continuous support, but relies on social interactions for this support. The Zone of Proximal Development - This is whereby a learner is provided with assistance from an educator to help reach their full learning potential. This may be material whereby students may be struggling, but they have reached this stage whereby the cognitive skills process can be identified by the educator.

There are many factors to consider with the social learning theory, with each aiding one another in the learning process:

  • Modelling Behaviour - as the learning is related to social interactions to aid with cognitive development, particular behaviours can have an influence. Students observe these behaviours and learn new concepts from observing one another. These behaviours relate to the educator and other students, thus positive relationships are essential for social learning theory.
  • Learning Environment - the educator must establish a positive learning environment that allows for social interactions.
  • Innate Person - the teacher and the students themselves contribute to the modelling behaviour and the establishment of the positive learning environment, without which social learning would not be possible.


Social Learning in a Computer Science Classroom:

  • Set tasks for each student. This can be based on their ability levels and can achieve some of the learning outcomes.
  • Set the success criteria: Should, Would and Could. This is extremely important to identify the cognitive process. In the lesson, I should be able to, would be able to and could be able to. The “could” represents students entering the zone of proximal development, which may be out of reach for some learners, but they can attain the “should” and “would”.
  • The classroom needs to be set up to allow for maximum collaboration. This may include adding students to collaborative Jamboards or Google Slides, or setting stations in the classroom to allow for discussions relating to their tasks.
  • The educator has an important role - to ensure that students are on task, monitor the learning, but also identify students entering the zone of proximal development.


Whole Brain Learning

Whole brain learning is a learning process that maximises student learning potential by utilising all aspects of the left and right side of the brain. The concept promotes engagement of all aspects and allows for differentiation throughout the learning activities, whereas traditional teaching strategies may only focus on one aspect.

The diagram represents the 4 types of learning aspects, which traditionally would have been referred to as the left and right side. Although students may be comfortable with a single quadrant, they may have some aspects of other quadrants. Educators should be mindful that students participating in only one quadrant may not be considered as whole brain learning. Lessons need to be designed to utilise all aspects of the brain, to promote engagement and maximise student potential.

Constructivist Theory

Constructivist theory has been explored by many scholars over the decades. It is an active process that focuses attention on the students developing their own understanding and learning based on knowledge already known to them (Sentance, 2017). It places emphasis on students' construction and connection with the knowledge as opposed to receiving and storing knowledge from the educator (Richardson, 2003).

Ben-Ari (1998) reports that applying constructivist theory has proved beneficial when exploring aspects, such as programming. As the theory places students at the centre of the learning, they are actively involved in constructing knowledge. This also allows for the development of a variety of skills, such as problem solving, critical thinking and computational thinking skills. The constructivist theory supports strategies that are active and engaging in a computer science classroom

This could be applied throughout many class activities. One such example is the application of programming using microbits. Students are familiar with programming using block based interface and/or Python language, but applying that knowledge to an artefact would have a higher impact on their learning. This is a short list of basic programming activities that allows students to construct based on their previous knowledge. Some of these may challenge sme students, whereas the opposite could also be true, depending on the class group.

  • Create a “FitBit” using microbits. Based on previous programming experience, students can apply this knowledge and construct their own versions of a Fitbit.
  • Create a bleep monitor system. Again, students can use technologies available in the room to create the system based on their experiences.
  • Create a game in Scratch - make advancements to the game based on their own experiences.


Many aspects of constructivism exist within other pedagogical approaches so it can be a difficult approach to isolate as its concepts are interlinked. Nonetheless, the ability of students to construct knowledge and skills based on their previous knowledge should be explored in a computer science classroom. It allows the development of social learning theory, increases engagement, maximises learning potential and allows students to take ownership of their work, thus leading to a better learning experience.



Unplugged Activities

Unplugged computing is essentially teaching computing concepts without using a computer (Bell, et al., 2009). These unplugged activities can provide a platform for many pedagogical approaches and can introduce concepts to coding and computer science without the computing concept. These activities are physical activities, rather than digital. They are active in design and can be used as an introductory concept to coding and computer science. It incorporates kinaesthetic approaches (learning by doing). Examples of unplugged activities can include role playing computation, puzzles, games, physical scratch blocks and more.

Benefits of Unplugged Activities:

  • Incorporates a kinaesthetic approach.
  • Involves physical abstracts rather than digital.
  • Encourages the development of skills without using technology.
  • Focuses students attention on concepts rather than technology.
  • Provides opportunities for discussions and active learning.




Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

This style of teaching and learning allows the incorporation of all learners to achieve their desired outcomes via differentiated learning pathways. It allows flexibility within the curriculum to provide opportunities for all students to learn in a preferred way that plays to their own strengths.

UDL is underpinned by 3 core principles: Engagement, Representation, Action and Expression. It aims to maximise student engagement via collaborative group work activities, by using a different variety of multimedia to help support learning material and ensuring that learners have a choice of assessment for the same learning outcome (Ahead, 2020).

Although some aspects of UDL may already exist in classrooms, awareness and practice is essential to ensure that all learners have access to the curriculum. Student choice of a pathway to achieving the outcome is essential to the success of UDL as it allows students to be engaged, take ownership of their learning and express their understanding of the learning outcome.

UDL in Computer Science

As UDL is associated with student choice of pathway and assessment, this can prove beneficial in a computer science classroom. The teacher can take any learning outcome and provide pathways for students to follow for their learning. The pathways take into consideration any learning difficulties that students may have so they can achieve the desired learning outcomes.
